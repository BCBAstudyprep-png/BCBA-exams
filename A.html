Question 1
Correct Answer: C. Control
Rationale
Behavior analysis has three goals: description (what occurred), prediction (when/where it is likely to occur again), and control (demonstrating a functional relation by manipulating variables to change behavior). Arranging an intervention that systematically changes behavior is the goal of control. Replication is a feature of single-case experimental design, not one of the three scientific goals.
A.1 — Goals of behavior analysis
Question 2
Correct Answer: B. Prediction
Rationale
Identifying a reliable correlation between events allows the analyst to predict when behavior is more likely to occur — but no variable has yet been manipulated, so control has not been demonstrated. Description involves recording the topography and circumstances of behavior; prediction involves identifying lawful relations between events that allow forecasting.
A.1 — Goals of behavior analysis
Question 3
Correct Answer: B. Description
Rationale
Description involves the systematic observation and recording of behavior, including operational definitions and the conditions under which behavior occurs. No correlations or functional relations have been demonstrated yet, so prediction and control have not been achieved.
A.1 — Goals of behavior analysis
Question 4
Correct Answer: C. The researcher manipulates an independent variable and produces systematic changes in the dependent variable.
Rationale
Control is demonstrated when the systematic manipulation of an independent variable reliably produces a change in the dependent variable, establishing a functional relation. Identifying antecedent-behavior correlations is prediction; operationally defining behavior is description.
A.1 — Goals of behavior analysis
Question 5
Correct Answer: B. Prediction
Rationale
The statement forecasts behavior based on an antecedent manipulation but does not represent that the manipulation has been done. Forecasting future behavior based on identified relations is prediction. Control would require actually implementing the run and demonstrating a systematic change.
A.1 — Goals of behavior analysis
Question 6
Correct Answer: C. Selectionism
Rationale
Selectionism is the assumption that behavior, like biological traits, is selected by its consequences over time — at the levels of phylogeny (species), ontogeny (individual learning), and cultural practices. The analogy to natural selection is the hallmark of selectionism.
A.2 — Philosophical assumptions
Question 7
Correct Answer: B. Parsimony
Rationale
Parsimony (also called the law of parsimony or Occam's razor) is the assumption that all simple, logical explanations of a phenomenon should be ruled out before more complex, hypothetical, or mentalistic explanations are considered.
A.2 — Philosophical assumptions
Question 8
Correct Answer: C. Determinism
Rationale
Determinism is the assumption that the universe is lawful and orderly — events (including behavior) do not occur randomly but are the product of identifiable causes. Without determinism, science could not exist, because phenomena could not be predicted or controlled.
A.2 — Philosophical assumptions
Question 9
Correct Answer: A. Empiricism
Rationale
Empiricism is the practice of objective observation and measurement of phenomena. Behavior analysis is empirical because it depends on direct, repeatable measurement of observable behavior rather than on opinion, intuition, or introspection.
A.2 — Philosophical assumptions
Question 10
Correct Answer: D. Pragmatism
Rationale
Pragmatism is the assumption that the truth or value of a concept is judged by how well it works in producing the desired outcomes — for behavior analysis, prediction and control of behavior. A theory that does not lead to effective action has little pragmatic value.
A.2 — Philosophical assumptions
Question 11
Correct Answer: B. Radical behaviorism
Rationale
Radical behaviorism, articulated by Skinner, holds that private events (thoughts, feelings, sensations) are behavior — they are subject to the same principles as public behavior and are appropriate subject matter for a science of behavior. Methodological behaviorism rejects private events as legitimate scientific data because they cannot be objectively observed.
A.3 — Radical behaviorism
Question 12
Correct Answer: B. Radical behaviorism includes private events as part of the subject matter of behavior analysis.
Rationale
Methodological behaviorism considers only publicly observable behavior to be the legitimate subject matter of science, treating private events as outside its scope. Radical behaviorism extends behavior analysis to private events (thoughts, feelings, sensations), treating them as behavior subject to the same principles — though they cannot be directly observed by others.
A.3 — Radical behaviorism
Question 13
Correct Answer: C. A verbal response describing a private event that is itself behavior.
Rationale
From a radical behaviorist perspective, the report is a verbal response (a tact of a private event). The private state itself — felt anxiety — is also behavior, controlled by environmental and physiological variables. Radical behaviorism does not treat private events as inner causes; it treats them as behavior to be explained, not as explanations themselves.
A.3 — Radical behaviorism
Question 14
Correct Answer: B. Is an example of explanatory fiction that does not identify environmental variables controlling the behavior.
Rationale
Radical behaviorism rejects mentalistic explanations that posit inner agents or hypothetical constructs (an "anger problem") as causes of behavior. These are explanatory fictions: they appear to explain but actually just rename the behavior and obscure the environmental variables responsible. The behavior analyst looks for environmental variables (antecedents and consequences) that account for the behavior.
A.3 — Radical behaviorism
Question 15
Correct Answer: C. Both public and private events are behavior and can be analyzed using the same principles.
Rationale
A defining feature of radical behaviorism is that it does not draw a fundamental distinction between public behavior and private events. Both are behavior; both are functions of environmental variables and learning history; both can be analyzed using the same principles. Option D describes methodological behaviorism.
A.3 — Radical behaviorism
Question 16
Correct Answer: B. The experimental analysis of behavior (EAB)
Rationale
The experimental analysis of behavior (EAB) is the basic-research branch of behavior analysis. It investigates fundamental behavioral processes, often in laboratory settings with non-human subjects, to identify basic principles. Behaviorism is the philosophy of the science; ABA applies the principles to socially significant behaviors; practice involves direct service delivery.
A.4 — Branches of behavior analysis
Question 17
Correct Answer: C. Applied behavior analysis
Rationale
Applied behavior analysis (ABA) is the branch of behavior analysis that applies the principles derived from EAB to behaviors of social significance, using research methods to improve socially important behavior. Teaching functional communication to a child with autism is a classic example of applied behavior-analytic research and intervention.
A.4 — Branches of behavior analysis
Question 18
Correct Answer: A. Behaviorism
Rationale
Behaviorism is the philosophy of the science of behavior — its underlying assumptions, goals, and worldview. EAB is the basic-research branch, ABA is the applied-research and intervention branch, and professional practice involves delivering services to consumers.
A.4 — Branches of behavior analysis
Question 19
Correct Answer: D. Professional practice guided by the science of behavior analysis
Rationale
Professional practice involves delivering behavior-analytic services to clients, applying the principles and procedures derived from EAB and ABA. While ABA as a research discipline involves the systematic study of socially significant behavior, day-to-day service delivery (treatment planning, supervision, intervention) is best described as practice guided by the science.
A.4 — Branches of behavior analysis
Question 20
Correct Answer: B. Behaviorism → EAB → ABA → professional practice
Rationale
Behaviorism is the philosophy underlying the science. EAB is the basic-research branch that identifies fundamental principles. ABA applies those principles to socially significant behavior through research. Professional practice delivers those evidence-based procedures to consumers. The flow is from philosophy to basic science to applied science to practice.
A.4 — Branches of behavior analysis
Question 21
Correct Answer: B. Applied
Rationale
The applied dimension means the behaviors selected for change must be socially significant to the participants — important to the individual's quality of life, not merely of theoretical interest. This dimension is what distinguishes ABA from EAB.
A.5 — Dimensions of ABA
Question 22
Correct Answer: A. Technological
Rationale
The technological dimension means that all procedures used in a study or intervention are identified and described with sufficient detail and clarity that a competent reader could replicate them. If steps are missing or vague, the procedure is not technological.
A.5 — Dimensions of ABA
Question 23
Correct Answer: C. Conceptually systematic
Rationale
Conceptually systematic means that interventions and procedures are described in terms of the basic principles of behavior from which they were derived (reinforcement, stimulus control, extinction, etc.). This dimension links applied practice to its scientific foundations and helps generalize findings across cases.
A.5 — Dimensions of ABA
Question 24
Correct Answer: B. Generality
Rationale
Generality means behavior change should last over time (maintenance), appear in environments other than where it was trained (setting/stimulus generalization), and spread to related behaviors (response generalization). Greeting adults across settings and over time exemplifies all of these.
A.5 — Dimensions of ABA
Question 25
Correct Answer: A. Applied, behavioral, analytic, technological, conceptually systematic, effective, generality
Rationale
The seven dimensions are: applied, behavioral, analytic, technological, conceptually systematic, effective, and generality. Option D lists the goals and philosophical assumptions; option C confuses dimensions with the branches of behavior analysis.
